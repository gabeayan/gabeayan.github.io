Baja SAE Vehicle Data Acquisition System

Before this project, the Stevens Baja car went into competition with essentially no quantitative data about how it was performing. We tuned by feel and guessed at brake bias. This project builds the foundation to change that — a modular, expandable DAQ system that logs sensor data throughout every testing session and competition run.

../images/daq-hero.jpg — DAQ electronics / Teensy setup

Why This Matters

The Stevens Baja SAE team does a lot of testing between competitions — CVT tuning, brake bias adjustment, suspension setup. Before this project, all of it was done qualitatively: how does the driver feel the car behaves? Does it feel like it's pulling? Does it feel like the brakes are balanced?

Qualitative feedback has limits. A driver can tell you "the car pushed in the right corner," but can't tell you that the rear left wheel locked up for 40 milliseconds before turn-in, which caused the understeer. Data can. The goal of this DAQ system is to give the team numbers — real numbers — to make decisions from.

Even in the short term, having GPS track logs, IMU data, and wheel speed lets us correlate driver feedback with actual vehicle dynamics, measure consistent top speed across setup changes, and catch electrical anomalies before they become failures at competition.

Architecture

The system is built around a Teensy 4.1 microcontroller. The Teensy 4.1 runs a 600 MHz ARM Cortex-M7 processor — far more computing power than needed for data logging, but that headroom means the system can take on more processing tasks in the future (real-time display, anomaly detection) without hardware changes.

Why Teensy 4.1 over Arduino?
The Teensy has 4 hardware serial ports (important for multiple GPS/IMU modules communicating simultaneously), native SD card support via the add-on board, and 3.3V logic that's compatible with all the sensors in the system without level shifters. It also has a substantially faster ADC than typical Arduino boards.

The architecture is hub-and-spoke: the Teensy is the hub, and each sensor is a spoke communicating via its native protocol. I chose this deliberately over a CAN bus or other fieldbus because it's simpler to implement, easier to debug, and sufficient for the data rates we need. If the system scales enough to justify a fieldbus, it can be redesigned then.

Sensors: Active

GPS

A GPS module logs position, heading, and speed at up to 10 Hz. This gives us: lap timing (comparing identical sections of the test track across setup changes), top speed measurement (a real number instead of a driver estimate), and eventually the ability to overlay other sensor data on a track map.

GPS update rate is the limiting factor here — 10 Hz is fine for position tracking but not fast enough to capture transient dynamics. That's where the IMU comes in.

IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit)

A 6-axis IMU (3-axis accelerometer + 3-axis gyroscope) runs at up to 1 kHz, giving us high-frequency data on:

  • Lateral acceleration — cornering g-force, tells us how hard the car is cornering
  • Longitudinal acceleration — braking and acceleration g-force
  • Vertical acceleration — suspension events, bumps, jumps
  • Roll, pitch, yaw rates — body dynamics during cornering and braking

This is one of the most valuable datasets on the car. Combined with GPS and wheel speed, it can tell us a great deal about what the suspension is doing and how the car is behaving dynamically.

Battery Voltage

A resistive voltage divider scales the 12V vehicle battery down to the Teensy's 3.3V ADC range. This gives us state-of-charge monitoring in real time, and can flag anomalies (voltage drops under load that might indicate a bad connection, for example).

Sensors: In Progress

Hall Effect Wheel Speed

Hall effect sensors detect the passage of ferrous teeth on a target ring attached to the wheel hub. By counting pulses and knowing the number of teeth, the Teensy can compute wheel speed in real time.

Wheel speed is one of the most important measurements on the car. Combined with GPS speed (vehicle speed), it lets us detect:

  • Wheel lock-up during braking — wheel speed drops to zero while GPS speed doesn't
  • Wheel spin during acceleration — wheel speed exceeds GPS speed
  • Differential behavior — comparing front and rear, or left and right speeds

I'm currently working on the target ring geometry (number of teeth, mounting to the hub) and the mounting geometry for the sensors to maintain consistent air gap as the suspension moves.

Sensors: Planned

Brake Pressure

A pressure transducer teed into each brake line measures hydraulic pressure, which is directly proportional to braking force at that axle. This lets us: measure how hard the driver is braking (as a number, not a feeling), tune front/rear brake bias quantitatively, and detect brake fade over a session.

Suspension Travel

A linear potentiometer on each corner measures suspension travel. Combined with the IMU data, this gives a complete picture of what the suspension is doing: how much travel is being used, whether it's bottoming out, how the dampers are responding to different terrain.

Housings & Packaging

All electronics are enclosed in custom 3D-printed housings. The housings need to survive the vibration and impact environment of off-road racing — which is significantly more demanding than a typical electronics enclosure application.

../images/daq-housing.jpg — 3D printed enclosure

Key design features of the housings:

  • Bolt-through mounting to the roll cage — not adhesive. Adhesive connections fail under sustained vibration.
  • Strain-relief grommets at all wire exits — preventing fatigue cracks at the conductor/insulation boundary
  • Snap-fit + fastener closures — quick access for maintenance, but won't vibrate open
  • Rubber isolation pads between the housing and the chassis — damps high-frequency vibration before it reaches the electronics
