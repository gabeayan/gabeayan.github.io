Two-Stage Reduction Gearbox Redesign

After a catastrophic failure during testing, I redesigned the Baja SAE gearbox from the ground up — applying gear stress analysis to understand why the previous one failed, then building a substantially stronger replacement with a permanent fix for the chronic oil leak problem.

../images/gearbox-hero.jpg — finished gearbox photo

Background

Baja SAE is a collegiate off-road racing competition. The Stevens car runs a stock Briggs & Stratton 10hp engine through a CVT (continuously variable transmission), and then through a reduction gearbox before the rear axle. The reduction gearbox is a critical component — it multiplies the torque from the CVT output shaft and steps down the speed to a range suitable for the wheels.

During testing, the previous gearbox failed catastrophically. By "catastrophically" I mean complete gear tooth fracture — not gradual wear, not a bearing failure, not a seal issue, but the gear teeth themselves snapping off under load. This is a sign that the gears were stressed beyond their ultimate strength, not just their yield strength, meaning the factor of safety on the previous design was dangerously low.

As Powertrain Lead, I was responsible for the redesign. The goals were clear: dramatically increase factor of safety, fix the chronic oil leaks that had plagued previous gearboxes, and document the analysis so future leads could understand the design rationale.

Gear Stress Analysis

Before designing anything new, I needed to understand what the previous gearbox was actually rated for — and why it failed. I used the Lewis equation for gear tooth bending stress, which models a gear tooth as a cantilever beam with a load applied at the tip:

Lewis Equation: σ = Wt / (F · m · Y)

Where: σ is the bending stress at the root of the tooth, Wt is the tangential load on the tooth, F is the face width, m is the module (tooth size), and Y is the Lewis form factor (depends on number of teeth and tooth geometry).

Working from the known inputs — the engine's peak torque (~18.5 ft-lb), the CVT ratio at full engagement, and the first-stage gear ratio — I calculated the maximum tangential force on the first-stage gear pair. Plugging this into the Lewis equation with the previous gearbox's gear dimensions gave me the bending stress the old gears were experiencing, and from that, the factor of safety against the material's allowable bending stress.

The result confirmed the failure: the old factor of safety was inadequate, and it was even lower once I accounted for impact loading. Off-road terrain — especially the obstacles in a Baja SAE competition — routinely produces shock loads 2–3× higher than the steady-state torque. A gearbox designed with a tight factor of safety on steady-state loads will fail eventually under peak off-road conditions.

For the redesign, I sized both gear stages to a substantially higher factor of safety, with explicit margin for the shock loading multiplier. I also ran the analysis on both stages separately, since the loading differs between the two mesh points.

Material Selection

Housing6061-T6 Aluminum — Excellent machinability, lightweight, corrosion resistant. The housing doesn't experience gear mesh loads directly; it supports bearing loads and maintains shaft alignment.
Gears4140 Chromoly Steel, heat-treated to HRC 50 — High strength steel with good hardenability. The heat treatment to HRC 50 meaningfully increases the allowable bending stress and dramatically improves surface fatigue life (pitting resistance).
BearingsDeep groove ball bearings sized for the radial and thrust loads at each shaft location
SealLaser-cut Buna-N (nitrile rubber) gasket — see Oil Leak section below
FastenersSteel cap screws, torqued to specification

The previous design used what appeared to be unhardened or minimally hardened steel. Hardening to HRC 50 moves the material into the range where gear tooth bending fatigue strength and surface durability are both substantially improved. The cost in machinability is acceptable because the gears can be roughed before heat treatment and finished-ground after.

Fixing the Oil Leak

Previous gearboxes on the Stevens Baja car had a persistent oil leak problem. The previous sealing method was RTV (room-temperature vulcanization) silicone applied as a bead around the mating housing faces before bolting together.

RTV works, but it has failure modes. It's applied as a bead of variable thickness, which means the compression is uneven across the sealing surface. It degrades with repeated thermal cycling. And there's no reproducible way to apply it — every assembly is slightly different.

The redesigned gearbox uses a Buna-N (nitrile rubber) gasket that was laser-cut to the exact geometry of the mating face. Nitrile rubber is the standard material for oil seals precisely because it's resistant to petroleum-based lubricants. Laser cutting gives a consistent, repeatable thickness with no variation between assemblies. The result is a defined, uniform compression around the entire sealing perimeter — and it's replaceable: if it ever needs to be recut, the same CAD file produces an identical replacement.

Prototyping

Before any metal was cut, I 3D-printed the housing design in PLA. This served as a fit check: I could press in dummy bearings, install the shafts, and verify that the gear spacing and bearing pocket geometry were correct. Assembly and disassembly procedure could also be worked out without worrying about damaging metal parts.

The prototype stage caught two issues. First, one bearing pocket was slightly undersized — the bearing pressed in with too much interference and the pocket had to be enlarged in CAD. Second, a bolt in the flange pattern created interference with a shaft during assembly; the bolt had to be relocated. Both were simple fixes in CAD, but would have been expensive mistakes in aluminum.

Machining & Final Assembly

The housing was machined on a knee mill by our Baja machinists. The main operations were: facing and squaring the housing halves, boring the bearing pockets to final diameter, drilling and tapping the bolt pattern, and cutting the gasket face to flatness.

The gears were cut on a gear hobber, roughed while still soft, then sent out for heat treatment. After heat treatment to HRC 50, the mating surfaces were finish-ground to achieve proper tooth geometry and surface finish. The finished gears were pressed and keyed onto their shafts.

Results

The redesigned gearbox was assembled with the Buna-N gasket, filled with gear oil, and run on the car through a full testing session without failure. No oil leaks were observed — a first for the Stevens Baja car's gearbox history.

The factor of safety on the new gear design is significantly higher than the failed design, and explicitly accounts for the shock loading typical of off-road competition. The full Lewis equation analysis has been documented and will be included in the team's design package for future reference.
