Automated Plant Watering System

Most automated plant waterers are functional but ugly — they look like engineering experiments on your windowsill. This project had two equal-weight goals: build a system that actually keeps plants healthy, and design an enclosure that looks like it belongs in someone's home.

../images/plant-hero.jpg — finished device photo

Design Brief

The technical brief was straightforward: build a device that automatically waters a plant and keeps it at an ideal moisture level without user intervention beyond occasionally refilling the water tank.

But I added a constraint that the brief didn't require: the enclosure should be designed so that a non-engineer walking into a room doesn't immediately identify it as electronics. It should look like it belongs next to the plant, not like it's monitoring it.

These two goals — functional accuracy and aesthetic integration — drove every design decision from sensor selection to housing geometry.

Why Multiple Sensors?

The naive approach to automatic plant watering is a single soil moisture sensor: if the soil is dry, water it. This works in a controlled environment, but fails in real conditions because soil moisture alone is an incomplete picture.

Consider: a plant sitting in direct afternoon sun on a hot, dry day needs water faster than the same plant in a north-facing window on a cool, humid day — even if the soil reads the same moisture level. The plant in the sun is losing water faster through its leaves via transpiration, and the soil is evaporating faster too. A single-sensor system can't see any of that.

The solution is to fuse multiple environmental signals into the watering decision:

Soil moisture Capacitive sensor — primary signal. Measures the volumetric water content of the soil. Capacitive sensors were chosen over resistive sensors because resistive sensors pass current through the soil, which slowly electrolyzes the probe tips over time. Capacitive sensors have no conductive parts in contact with the soil.
Temperature & humidity DHT22 — hot, dry air increases plant transpiration rate. At high temperature and low relative humidity, plants lose water faster through their stomata. This shortens the interval before the next watering is needed.
Ambient light Phototransistor — light intensity correlates with photosynthesis rate and temperature. High light means more metabolic activity, more growth, and more water demand.

Watering Algorithm

The watering decision logic uses the soil moisture reading as the primary trigger, with the environmental sensors adjusting the threshold dynamically:

  • A base soil moisture threshold triggers watering when the soil gets too dry
  • When temperature is high and humidity is low (high vapor pressure deficit, or VPD), the threshold is raised slightly — the system waters more preemptively before the soil gets as dry as it otherwise would
  • When light levels are high (indicating the plant is actively photosynthesizing), the threshold shifts in the same direction
  • At night or in low-light conditions, the threshold is relaxed — the plant isn't transpiring much, so there's less urgency

This is a simplified version of the evapotranspiration models used in precision agriculture, adapted to work with inexpensive sensors and without full weather station data.

Water Delivery

Water is delivered by a small peristaltic pump. The choice of peristaltic over a centrifugal or gear pump was deliberate:

  • Self-priming — works even after sitting idle for days without needing to re-prime
  • No metal parts in contact with water — avoids contamination and corrosion
  • Consistent volume per pulse count — by counting motor pulses, the firmware knows exactly how much water was delivered without needing a flow sensor
  • No minimum flow rate — can deliver small amounts precisely

The pump draws from a reservoir integrated into the base of the enclosure. The water volume in a single watering event is calculated from the soil moisture reading: a very dry plant gets a larger dose, a plant that's only slightly under threshold gets a small one.

Enclosure Design

This is the part I spent the most time on, and the part I'm most proud of.

The design brief I gave myself: a non-engineer walking into a room should not immediately recognize this as an electronics project. It should read as a decorative object, appropriate for a windowsill with other plants.

The solution was to design the housing to resemble a sculptural succulent — a layered, organic form with a visual language that fits the context of a plant shelf. This wasn't an afterthought applied to an existing electronics box; the aesthetic was the starting constraint, and the electronics were packaged to fit it.

../images/plant-sketch.jpg — early design sketches or CAD explorations

The housing prints in three parts:

  • Upper decorative form — the "plant" shape. The leaf forms conceal the routing of the soil probe wires down into the pot. Printed in a matte green filament.
  • Mid-ring — sits at the junction between the decorative form and the pot. Contains a frosted diffuser ring for the LED feedback strip. The LEDs pulse slowly when the tank is low — visible but not intrusive.
  • Base / tank section — houses the water reservoir, pump, and electronics. Designed so the whole assembly lifts cleanly off the pot for tank refilling without disconnecting any wires.

LED Feedback

The LED feedback system serves one purpose: tell the user when the water tank needs refilling. The design goal was "noticeable without being annoying."

The LEDs are diffused through the frosted mid-ring, which spreads the light into a soft ambient glow rather than a harsh point source. They pulse with a slow breathing pattern (fade in over 2 seconds, hold, fade out over 2 seconds) — a pattern that reads as "this needs attention" without being urgent or distracting.

When the tank is sufficiently full, the LEDs are off entirely. They only activate when the reservoir drops below 20% capacity, giving the user a comfortable window to refill before the system runs dry.

Results

The device was tested over a four-week period with a pothos plant. The plant maintained healthy soil moisture levels throughout without any manual watering. The LED feedback triggered twice over the period, at which point refilling the tank took about 30 seconds.

The enclosure design achieved its aesthetic goal: several people who saw it on the testing shelf initially assumed it was just a decorative object and didn't notice the soil probe or the LED ring until pointed out.
