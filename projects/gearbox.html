Two-Stage Reduction Gearbox

At the end of March, the team's stopgap transfer case failed at testing: gear teeth sheared off the intermediate shaft. I took over as Powertrain Lead. In roughly two days I designed a replacement gearbox from scratch, sized the gears using Lewis equation analysis, fixed the chronic oil leak with a laser-cut Buna-N gasket, and personally cut both 19-tooth pinion gears on the Bridgeport. The gearbox ran through a full campus testing season without failure.

images/gearbox-gear-health.jpg

Overview

The team had been running a transfer case pulled from a prior-year car as a stopgap. It failed at testing because its pinion gears were only 15 teeth, too small for the diametral pitch, giving a Lewis factor of safety of about 0.4. I took over and in two days designed a replacement with 19-tooth gears, 4140 steel heat-treated to HRC 50, and a laser-cut gasket. The new design has a FoS of 0.865 at the theoretical worst-case load — full engine stall — a condition the car never reaches in operation. Zero failures and zero oil leaks through the entire testing season.

Background

Baja SAE is a collegiate off-road racing competition run by SAE International. Teams design and build a single-seat off-road vehicle around a spec engine — a single cylinder making about 10 hp — and compete in events from a drag race-style acceleration run to a 4-hour endurance race. This year, Stevens competes as car #89.

Failure Analysis

The transfer case failed through complete gear tooth fracture on the intermediate shaft. This was a fast-fracture failure: the material was loaded past its ultimate strength in a single event rather than fatiguing gradually. Teardown identified two contributing factors.

The pinion gears in that unit were 15 teeth, well below the 19-tooth pinions in the redesign. At the same diametral pitch (10), a smaller tooth count raises bending stress in two ways. First, the pitch radius is smaller, so the same torque produces a larger tangential force on each tooth. Second, fewer teeth means a lower Lewis form factor Y, indicating higher stress concentration at the tooth root. Both effects push stress in the same direction and they multiply.

Effect of tooth count on bending stress — same torque, same DP, same face width
15T pinion: r = 15/20 = 0.750 in, Y = 0.276
19T pinion: r = 19/20 = 0.950 in, Y = 0.314

σ = (Wt × P) / (F × Y)

Stress ratio (same torque): σ15T / σ19T = (r19 × Y19) / (r15 × Y15) = (0.950 × 0.314) / (0.750 × 0.276) = 0.2983 / 0.2070 = 1.44

A 15-tooth pinion at DP 10 sees about 44% higher bending stress than a 19-tooth pinion carrying the same torque.

The transfer case gears were also heat treated to approximately HRC 40 and had been sitting in storage for months before installation. Lewis analysis gives a factor of safety of about 0.4 under the worst-case static load — a gearbox at 40% of its required strength survives in storage, but not under competition drivetrain loads.

Design Approach

The 55-tooth and 57-tooth driven gears had already been ordered and manufactured. Working from that constraint, I chose 19-tooth pinions — an increase from the 15-tooth gears that had failed. Beyond fixing the FoS, the larger pinion also produces a more favorable speed ratio. The car had consistently struggled to reach competitive top speeds, and the 19-tooth mesh reduces the overall gear reduction compared to the 15-tooth setup.

Before committing to machining, I built a full CAD assembly and imported bearing and shaft seal models from McMaster-Carr to verify fit and clearances. Getting everything checked at the assembly level meant no surprises during manufacturing.

One key step was deriving center distances directly from the gear geometry rather than relying on inherited values. For an involute spur gear mesh, center distance is set entirely by tooth count and diametral pitch:

Required center distances — derived from gear geometry
C = (Tdriver + Tdriven) / (2P)

Stage 1 (19T → 57T): C₁ = (19 + 57) / (2 × 10) = 3.800 in
Stage 2 (19T → 55T): C₂ = (19 + 55) / (2 × 10) = 3.700 in

These values set the bearing bore locations in the housing model. Getting center distance wrong forces gears off their pitch circles: too tight causes interference and elevated contact stress, too loose creates excessive backlash and impact loads each time a tooth pair comes into mesh.

Drivetrain Load Analysis

Before sizing any gear, I traced the torque path from the engine to the axle under the worst-case condition: engine at peak torque, CVT at its most torque-biased ratio, wheels completely stationary. This is the highest load that can appear at any mesh point.

Stage Ratio Torque In Torque Out
CVT (Comet-790) 3.38:1 18.5 ft·lb 62.5 ft·lb
Stage 1 (19T → 57T) 3.00:1 62.5 ft·lb 187.5 ft·lb
Stage 2 (19T → 55T) 2.89:1 187.5 ft·lb ~542 ft·lb

Total gearbox reduction: 3.0 × 2.89 = 8.68:1. Total drivetrain at peak CVT ratio: 3.38 × 8.68 = about 29.3:1. The Stage 2 input pinion carries the highest tangential force in the system — it sits on the intermediate shaft after the 3:1 Stage 1 multiplication, so its load is 9 times engine torque.

Gear Stress Analysis

The Lewis equation treats a gear tooth as a cantilever beam loaded at the tip. It is a standard conservative model for bending stress at the tooth root:

σ = (Wt × P) / (F × Y)

σ = root bending stress (psi)  ·  Wt = tangential force (lb)  ·  P = diametral pitch  ·  F = face width (in)  ·  Y = Lewis form factor

All gears: diametral pitch 10, face width 0.375 in, 20° full-depth involute. Y = 0.314 for 19 teeth at 20° from the standard Lewis table. Worst-case load at the Stage 2 pinion under full stall:

Stage 2 Pinion — Full Stall Load
Tin = 222 in·lb × 3.38 (CVT) × 3.0 (Stage 1) = 2,251 in·lb
rpinion = 19 / (2 × 10) = 0.950 in
Wt = 2,251 / 0.950 = 2,369.6 lb

σ = (2,369.6 × 10) / (0.375 × 0.314) = 201,236 psi

Allowable bending stress at HRC 50: 174,045 psi
Lewis FoS = 174,045 / 201,236 = 0.865

The FoS of 0.865 is against the theoretical maximum load: full drivetrain stall with the engine at peak torque and the CVT locked at its lowest ratio. This condition is never sustained in operation. The CVT begins to upshift before stall, tire slip limits torque transfer, and the engine unloads before reaching peak torque at stall RPM. The Lewis equation is also conservative by design — it assumes full load at a single tooth tip and ignores load sharing between adjacent teeth.

Materials

Housing 6061-T6 aluminum, Type II anodized. The housing carries bearing loads and maintains shaft alignment; it is not part of the gear mesh. 6061 machines easily and is strong enough for the structural loads here. Machined on the Fanuc CNC mill and anodized in the team's magenta color, which also adds a hard oxide layer for corrosion resistance.
Gears 4140 chromoly steel, HRC 50. 4140 through-hardens at these section sizes and stays tough at high hardness. Specifying HRC 50 rather than the HRC 40 of the old transfer case moved the allowable bending stress from about 130,000 psi to 174,045 psi.
Bearings Deep groove ball bearings at each of the six shaft locations, sized for the combined radial and thrust loads at each position.
Gasket Laser-cut Buna-N nitrile rubber. See oil seal section below.

CAD

The gearbox was fully modeled before any machining began. The three-shaft layout is visible in the orthographic front view: input shaft at top, intermediate shaft at center, output shaft at bottom. The exploded view was used during design review to confirm assembly sequence and verify clearances, with bearings and shaft seals imported from McMaster-Carr.

Prototyping

Before milling aluminum, I 3D-printed half of the housing shell to check mounting fit on the car's frame. The goal was to verify mounting tab geometry and position within the chassis: whether the tabs aligned with the correct frame nodes, whether the gearbox sat in the right position relative to the CVT output shaft and the axle, and whether there was clearance to surrounding structure. A physical check catches mounting problems that are easy to miss in CAD, especially when the frame model is assembled from multiple subteam files.

Machining

I handled all design and CAD work and specified every part before any machining began. Manufacturing was split across several processes.

The transfer case halves were machined on the team's Fanuc CNC mill. I helped run the machine for one half but didn't write the CAM. The case required two operations; to set up the second, I made a fixture plate on the Bridgeport manual mill to hold the case at the correct reference position. The second image below shows one half after the first operation, before that second setup.

The bearing carrier was turned on the CNC lathe, which I ran. The first image below shows it mid-operation in an unfinished state. Both 19-tooth pinion gears — the input shaft pinion and the intermediate shaft pinion — were cut on the Bridgeport, which I also operated. The two large driven gears, the 57-tooth and 55-tooth, were outsourced to an external shop specializing in gear manufacturing; cutting teeth at that size requires dedicated equipment we don't have in-house.

Assembly required press fits throughout. Gears were pressed onto shafts that had polygonal profiles cut into them for torque transfer. The polygon interface distributes torque through form contact across the shaft cross-section rather than concentrating it at a single keyway.

One quality issue came up during manufacturing: the input shaft came back from heat treatment at HRC 17 instead of the specified HRC 50. That result flagged a material error immediately — 1018 steel can't reach HRC 50 regardless of heat treatment, so the shaft had been machined from the wrong stock. A replacement was made from 4140 and sent for heat treatment. The 1018 shaft was run during campus testing while the correct part was being processed, then swapped out before competition.

Fixing the Oil Leak

Every previous Stevens gearbox used RTV silicone applied as a bead around the mating housing faces. RTV degrades with thermal cycling, and compression depends on how carefully it was applied that day. There is no way to get a consistent joint from a hand-applied bead. Every previous gearbox leaked.

The new design uses a Buna-N nitrile rubber gasket laser-cut from the same CAD file that defined the housing mating face. Every bolt hole is in the exact correct position. Nitrile is standard for oil seals: petroleum-resistant and stable across the operating temperature range. Compression is set by the material spec and housing flatness, not by whoever assembled it that day. The same CAD file produces an identical replacement on demand.

Results

The gearbox ran through a full campus testing season without mechanical failure, oil leaks, or any sign of tooth distress. It was the first gearbox on this car to leave a testing session without leaking oil.

Testing included full-throttle acceleration runs and braking at speed on campus.

After the testing season we disassembled the gearbox to check gear condition. The photo below shows the housing opened on the welding table: all four tooth surfaces intact with no signs of fatigue or plastic deformation, and gear oil still coating the mesh. The gasket had deformed slightly from repeated assembly cycles, so we laser-cut a replacement from the same CAD file.
